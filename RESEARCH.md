## 1. Foundations: Notes & the Fretboard

### The 12 notes
Western music uses **12 pitch classes** per octave. Using sharps (♯) and flats (♭), they are:

```
C  C♯/D♭  D  D♯/E♭  E  F  F♯/G♭  G  G♯/A♭  A  A♯/B♭  B  (→ C)
```

- **Natural notes:** C D E F G A B (7 notes — the white keys on a piano).
- **Accidentals:** the 5 notes between them, each with two names (enharmonic equivalents: C♯ = D♭).
- **The octave:** after 12 semitones you return to the same letter name one octave higher. The octave doubles the frequency (ratio 2:1).

### Why numbering stops at 7
Western scales are built from **7 notes** (heptatonic). We number scale degrees 1–7, then 8 = octave (= 1 again). This is why intervals go up to a 7th, and why **9 = 2, 11 = 4, 13 = 6** (the same note an octave higher, used when stacked above a 7th).

### The guitar fretboard
- A guitar fret is a **semitone**: moving up 1 fret raises the pitch by 1 semitone; up 2 frets = 1 whole tone.
- **Standard tuning** E-A-D-G-B-E. The interval between adjacent strings is a perfect 4th **except** between G and B, which is a major 3rd. This "kink" is the single most important fact shaping guitar chord shapes.
- **Octave shapes:** any note repeats 12 frets up the same string, and also across strings in predictable patterns (e.g., a note on the 6th string appears 2 frets higher on the 4th string, and 3 frets higher on the 5th string → 2nd). Learning octave shapes lets you find any note anywhere.
- **Frets 0–12** contain every note once; 12–24 repeat the pattern an octave up.
- **Open strings** = fret 0 (E A D G B E).

### Finding any note
Strategy: learn the notes on strings 6 and 5 first (they're the roots of most chord shapes). Every other note can be reached via octave shapes. The notes at fret 12 are the open-string names.

## 2. Intervals: The Building Blocks of Harmony

An **interval** is the distance between two notes. We measure it in **semitones (half steps / frets)**. There are 12 intervals within an octave.

| Semitones | Name | Symbol | Quality / character |
|-----------|------|--------|---------------------|
| 0 | Unison | 1 | same note |
| 1 | Minor 2nd | b2 | tense, dissonant |
| 2 | Major 2nd | 2 | step |
| 3 | Minor 3rd | b3 | "minor" sound (sad) |
| 4 | Major 3rd | 3 | "major" sound (happy) |
| 5 | Perfect 4th | 4 / 11 | open, suspended |
| 6 | Tritone (aug 4th / dim 5th) | #4 / b5 | dissonant, "the devil's interval" |
| 7 | Perfect 5th | 5 | stable, power chord |
| 8 | Minor 6th | b6 / b13 | dark |
| 9 | Major 6th | 6 / 13 | bright, "sixth chord" |
| 10 | Minor 7th | b7 | bluesy, dominant |
| 11 | Major 7th | 7 | jazzy, "major 7 chord" |
| 12 | Octave | 8 / 1 | same note, higher |

### Key concepts
- A **major interval** made one semitone smaller becomes **minor** (e.g., major 3rd → minor 3rd).
- A **perfect interval** (4th, 5th, octave) made one semitone smaller becomes **diminished**; one semitone larger becomes **augmented**.
- The **3rd** (major or minor) is the most important interval in harmony — it defines whether a chord is major or minor.
- The **tritone** (6 semitones) splits the octave exactly in half. It is the engine of dominant tension and tritone substitution (see §16).
- **Compound intervals:** 9th = 2nd + octave, 11th = 4th + octave, 13th = 6th + octave. These appear when you keep stacking thirds past the 7th.

### Why intervals matter
Every chord is a stack of intervals. Every scale is a sequence of intervals. If you understand intervals, you understand chords and scales — they are not separate topics.

## 3. Scales: Major, Minor & Beyond

### The major scale
The major scale is an agreed pattern of **whole (W) and half (H) steps**:

```
W - W - H - W - W - W - H
1     2  3  4     5  6  7  (8=1)
```

Example — **C major** (no sharps/flats): C D E F G A B C
Example — **G major** (1 sharp, F♯): G A B C D E F♯ G

The major scale is the reference for **everything**: intervals are named by comparing to the major scale, chord formulas are written against it, modes are derived from it.

### The natural minor scale (Aeolian)
Pattern: W - H - W - W - H - W - W. Formula relative to major: **1 2 b3 4 5 b6 b7**.
Example — **A natural minor** (relative to C major, same notes): A B C D E F G A.

### The harmonic minor scale
Natural minor with a **raised 7th** (to create a leading tone and a strong V chord).
Formula: **1 2 b3 4 5 b6 7**. Example: A B C D E F G♯ A.
- Purpose: gives a major V chord (E7) that resolves strongly to the minor tonic (Am). The raised 7th creates an exotic augmented-2nd gap between b6 and 7.

### The melodic minor scale (jazz)
In jazz, "melodic minor" = natural minor with **raised 6th AND 7th**: **1 2 b3 4 5 6 7**. Example: A B C D E F♯ G♯ A.
- This is the **ascending** form only (classical music lowers both on descent; jazz keeps them up).
- Melodic minor is the source of several crucial jazz modes (Lydian dominant, altered, etc. — see §25).

### The major pentatonic & minor pentatonic
- **Major pentatonic:** 1 2 3 5 6 (major scale without 4 and 7).
- **Minor pentatonic:** 1 b3 4 5 b7 (natural minor without 2 and 6).
- The **blues scale** = minor pentatonic + b5: 1 b3 4 b5 5 b7.

## 4. The Seven Modes of the Major Scale

A **mode** is a scale played from a note other than its root — i.e., you take the same 7 notes but treat a different degree as "home." Each mode has a **characteristic note** that gives it its flavor.

| Degree | Mode | Formula (vs major) | Characteristic note | Sound |
|--------|------|--------------------|---------------------|-------|
| I | **Ionian** | 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 | — | major scale; bright, happy |
| II | **Dorian** | 1 2 b3 4 5 6 b7 | natural 6 (vs minor) | minor but hopeful/bittersweet |
| III | **Phrygian** | 1 b2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 | b2 | dark, Spanish/flamenco |
| IV | **Lydian** | 1 2 3 #4 5 6 7 | #4 | dreamy, bright, "floating" |
| V | **Mixolydian** | 1 2 3 4 5 6 b7 | b7 | bluesy, dominant, rock |
| VI | **Aeolian** | 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 | b6 | natural minor; sad, somber |
| VII | **Locrian** | 1 b2 b3 4 b5 b6 b7 | b5 | unstable, dark, unresolved |

### How to think about modes (two views)
1. **Parent-scale view:** D Dorian = the notes of C major, starting on D. Easiest to construct.
2. **Parallel/formula view:** D Dorian = D major with b3 and b7. Easiest to play over a single chord.

### Practical mode usage (chord-scale matching)
- **Dorian** → over a iim7 chord, or any minor vamp (So What, Footprints). "If in doubt on a minor chord, play Dorian."
- **Mixolydian** → over any dominant 7 chord (the V of a key, or blues tonic).
- **Lydian** → over a Imaj7 that is the IV of the key, or any maj7 with a #11.
- **Aeolian** → over a minor tonic chord (natural minor).
- **Phrygian dominant** (a mode of harmonic minor, see §25) → over a V7 resolving to a minor tonic (flamenco/Middle-Eastern sound).
- **Locrian** → over a m7b5 (half-diminished) chord; rarely a tonal center.

## 5. Triads: The First Chords

A **triad** is a 3-note chord built by **stacking thirds** (skip every other scale degree). It contains a **root, a 3rd, and a 5th**. The quality of the 3rd and 5th determines the triad type.

### The four triad qualities

| Triad | Formula | Stack of 3rds | Example (C) | Sound |
|-------|---------|---------------|-------------|-------|
| **Major** | 1 3 5 | M3 + m3 | C E G | happy, stable |
| **Minor** | 1 b3 5 | m3 + M3 | C E♭ G | sad, stable |
| **Diminished** | 1 b3 b5 | m3 + m3 | C E♭ G♭ | tense, unstable |
| **Augmented** | 1 3 #5 | M3 + M3 | C E G♯ | suspenseful, ambiguous |

- **Major 3rd** = 4 semitones; **minor 3rd** = 3 semitones.
- Major triad: root→M3→m3. Minor: root→m3→M3. Diminished: root→m3→m3. Augmented: root→M3→M3.
- On guitar, triads appear as **small 3-note shapes** on adjacent string sets (strings 1-2-3, 2-3-4, 3-4-5, 4-5-6). Learning triads in all inversions across the neck is the gateway to fretboard freedom.

### Inversions
A triad can be voiced with any of its three notes in the bass:
- **Root position:** root in bass (1-3-5)
- **1st inversion:** 3rd in bass (3-5-1)
- **2nd inversion:** 5th in bass (5-1-3)

Same notes, different sound and bass motion. Inversions are the basis of smooth voice leading.

## 6. Seventh Chords

A **seventh chord** adds one more third on top of a triad — now 4 notes: root, 3rd, 5th, 7th. Seventh chords are the foundation of jazz harmony.

### The five essential 7th chords

| Chord | Formula | Stack of 3rds | Example (C) | Use |
|-------|---------|---------------|-------------|-----|
| **Major 7** (maj7) | 1 3 5 7 | M3 m3 M3 | C E G B | Imaj7, IVmaj7 |
| **Dominant 7** (7) | 1 3 5 b7 | M3 m3 m3 | C E G B♭ | V7 (resolves to I) |
| **Minor 7** (m7) | 1 b3 5 b7 | m3 M3 m3 | C E♭ G B♭ | iim7, vim7, im7 |
| **Half-diminished** (m7♭5 / ø) | 1 b3 b5 b7 | m3 m3 M3 | C E♭ G♭ B♭ | iim7b5 in minor ii–V–i |
| **Diminished 7** (dim7) | 1 b3 b5 bb7 | m3 m3 m3 | C E♭ G♭ B♭♭ | passing, leading-tone, vii° |

### How to build any 7th chord (3-step process)
1. Find the root note on the fretboard.
2. On the same string, count up the stack of thirds (major/minor) to find the 3rd, 5th, and 7th notes.
3. Find all occurrences of those 4 notes across the other strings — any combination (with doublings) forms a voicing.

### Why dominant 7 is special
The dominant 7 chord (1 3 5 b7) contains a **tritone** between the 3rd and b7th. This tritone creates **maximum tension** that wants to resolve: the 3rd pulls up by semitone, the b7 pulls down by semitone. This resolution (V7 → I) is the engine of all Western tonal harmony. It is also why tritone substitution works (see §16).

## 7. Extended & Altered Chords (9, 11, 13)

Once you have a 7th chord, you can keep stacking thirds to get **extensions**: the 9th, 11th, and 13th. These add color and richness, especially in jazz.

### Extensions = scale degrees an octave up
- **9th** = 2nd (above the 7th)
- **11th** = 4th (above the 9th)
- **13th** = 6th (above the 11th)

A theoretical 13th chord contains all 7 notes of the scale. In practice, guitarists omit notes (usually the 5th, sometimes the root) to make them playable.

### Common extended chords
| Symbol | Meaning | Notes (C root) |
|--------|---------|----------------|
| Cmaj9 | maj7 + 9 | C E G B D |
| C9 | dom7 + 9 | C E G B♭ D |
| Cm9 | m7 + 9 | C E♭ G B♭ D |
| Cmaj13 | maj7 + (9) + 13 | C E B D A (G optional) |
| C13 | dom7 + (9) + (11) + 13 | C E B♭ D A |
| Cm11 | m7 + 11 | C E♭ G B♭ D F |

### Altered dominant chords
Dominant 7 chords can have **altered** extensions (notes not in the parent major scale) to increase tension before resolution:

| Alteration | Degree | Effect |
|------------|--------|--------|
| ♭9 | b9 (1 semitone above root) | tense, dark |
| ♯9 | #9 | bluesy ("Hendrix chord": 7#9) |
| ♯11 | #4/#11 | lydian dominant, whole-tone-ish |
| ♭13 | b13/b6 | dark, resolves to 3rd of tonic |
| ♭5 / ♯5 | altered 5th | unstable, altered scale territory |

A chord marked **"alt"** or **"7alt"** means "play altered extensions" (typically b9 and/or #9, b5/#5, b13). The default scale is the **altered scale** (see §20).

### The "shell + extension" method (guitar)
A practical jazz-guitar approach:
1. Play a **shell voicing** (root, 3rd, 7th — the essential notes).
2. Add **one** extension on top for color.
3. Natural 9, 11, 13 are "inside"; b9, #9, #11, b13 are "altered."

Not every extension works on every chord (e.g., a natural 11 on a major 7 clashes with the 3rd — use #11 instead, i.e., Lydian). When in doubt: if it sounds good, it is good; experiment.

## 8. Diatonic Harmony: Chords Within a Key

**Diatonic** = "within the key." Diatonic chords are built **only from the notes of one scale**. They naturally sound like they belong together because they share the same note pool.

### Building diatonic 7th chords from the major scale
Take each scale degree as a root and **stack thirds** (skip every other note). In **C major** (C D E F G A B):

| Degree | Chord | Notes | Quality | Roman numeral |
|--------|-------|-------|---------|---------------|
| I | Cmaj7 | C E G B | Major 7 | **I** |
| II | Dm7 | D F A C | Minor 7 | **ii** |
| III | Em7 | E G B D | Minor 7 | **iii** |
| IV | Fmaj7 | F A C E | Major 7 | **IV** |
| V | G7 | G B D F | Dominant 7 | **V** |
| VI | Am7 | A C E G | Minor 7 | **vi** |
| VII | Bm7♭5 | B D F A | Half-diminished | **vii°** |

### The magic sequence
In **every major key**, the 7 diatonic 7th chords always come out as:
**maj7 – m7 – m7 – maj7 – dom7 – m7 – m7♭5**
or, as triads: **major – minor – minor – major – major – minor – diminished**.

This is not arbitrary — it falls out of the W-W-H-W-W-W-H pattern of the major scale. The varying sizes of the thirds between scale degrees produce different chord qualities.

### Roman numerals
- **Uppercase** (I, IV, V) = major.
- **Lowercase** (ii, iii, vi) = minor.
- **Lowercase + °** (vii°) = diminished.
- **Lowercase + ø** (iiø) = half-diminished.

Roman numerals let you describe a progression **in any key**: "I–IV–V–I" means the same relationship whether you're in C (C-F-G-C) or A (A-D-E-A). This is the key to transposing.

### Minor key diatonic chords
From the natural minor scale (e.g., A minor: A B C D E F G), the diatonic 7th chords are:
i7 – iiø7 – bIIImaj7 – iv7 – vm7 – bVImaj7 – bVII7
(Am7 – Bm7♭5 – Cmaj7 – Dm7 – Em7 – Fmaj7 – G7)

Note: the v chord is **minor** in natural minor (Em7), which lacks the strong dominant pull. That's why harmonic minor raises the 7th to give a **major V** (E7 → Am) — see §9.

### Relative major / relative minor
A major key and its relative minor **share the same notes and the same diatonic chords**, just with a different tonal center. C major's relative minor is A minor (vi = i). The vi chord of the major key is the i chord of the relative minor.

## 9. Functional Harmony: Tonic, Subdominant, Dominant

**Functional harmony** explains *why* chords behave the way they do — which feel stable, which create motion, and which demand resolution. Most tonal music relies on three harmonic **functions**:

| Function | Role | Chords (in C major) | Feeling |
|----------|------|---------------------|---------|
| **Tonic (T)** | Home, rest, resolution | I (C), vi (Am), iii (Em) | stable, settled |
| **Subdominant (S)** | Departure, lift, motion | IV (F), ii (Dm) | moving away |
| **Dominant (D)** | Tension, wants to resolve to I | V (G), vii° (B°) | tense, pulling home |

### The harmonic narrative
A typical progression tells a story: **leave home (T → S), build tension (S → D), return home (D → T)**. This tension-and-release cycle is the emotional engine of tonal music.

- **Tonic** chords feel at rest. The I is the strongest; vi and iii are "tonic substitutes" — stable but colored.
- **Subdominant** chords move away from home. IV lifts; ii (pre-dominant) sets up the dominant.
- **Dominant** chords contain the tritone (3rd + b7th of V7) that pulls toward the tonic. V7 → I is the strongest resolution in tonal music.

### The three functions as families (quick reference, C major)
- **Tonic:** I, vi (and iii)
- **Subdominant / Pre-dominant:** IV, ii (and vi)
- **Dominant:** V, vii° (and secondary dominants)

### Minor keys: borrowing a dominant
In natural minor, the v chord is minor (Em in Am) and lacks pull. Composers **borrow from harmonic minor** to get a major V (E7 in Am). The raised 7th (G♯) creates a leading tone that resolves up to the tonic A. Example: "House of the Rising Sun" — Am C D F Am C **E** Am.

## 10. The Circle of Fifths

The **circle of fifths** arranges the 12 pitch classes in a cycle of **perfect fifths** (clockwise) / perfect fourths (counterclockwise). Starting at C (top): **C → G → D → A → E → B → F♯ → C♯/D♭ → A♭ → E♭ → B♭ → F → C**.

### What it shows
1. **Key signatures:** each step clockwise adds a sharp; each step counterclockwise adds a flat. C = 0, G = 1♯, D = 2♯, … F = 1♭, B♭ = 2♭, …
2. **Closely related keys:** neighbors on the circle share the most notes (differ by only one sharp/flat). This is where modulations naturally go.
3. **The I–IV–V of any key:** find your key; the note to the **left is the IV**, the note to the **right is the V**.
4. **Relative minor:** the inner ring. The relative minor sits at the 6 o'clock-ish position relative to its major (e.g., C major ↔ A minor). They share a key signature.
5. **The full chord family:** to find all diatonic chords of a key, start at the IV (one left) and count 7 positions clockwise — those 7 notes are your scale. First 3 are major (I, IV, V), next 3 are minor (ii, vi, iii), last is diminished (vii°).

### Practical guitar uses
- **Transposing:** learn a progression as numbers (I–vi–IV–V), then apply it to any key by reading positions off the circle.
- **Finding the 1-4-5** for a blues in any key instantly.
- **Finding relative minors** for color chords.
- **Borrowed chords:** chords from the parallel minor (bIII, bVI, bVII) sit on the opposite side of the circle.
- **Secondary dominants:** the V of any chord is one step clockwise from it (see §15).

### The diatonic circle of fifths
Within a single key, the 7 diatonic chords also form a (partial) circle: …iii–vi–ii–V–I–IV… This is why progressions like I–vi–ii–V (a "circle progression") sound so smooth — each chord's root is a 4th above (or 5th below) the next, the most natural root motion in tonal music.

## 11. Chord Progressions & Roman Numerals

A **chord progression** is a sequence of chords. Describing them with Roman numerals reveals the underlying structure that works in any key.

### Foundational progressions (major key)
| Progression | In C major | Character | Genre |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|-------|
| **I–IV–V–I** | C–F–G–C | classic, strong | rock, country, folk |
| **I–V–vi–IV** | C–G–Am–F | emotional arc | pop (the "4-chord song") |
| **I–vi–IV–V** | C–Am–F–G | 50s doo-wop | pop, ballad |
| **ii–V–I** | Dm–G–C | jazz cadence | jazz, soul |
| **I–vi–ii–V** | C–Am–Dm–G | circle / turnaround | jazz, pop |
| **vi–IV–I–V** | Am–F–C–G | minor-starting pop | modern pop |

### Why diatonic progressions "work"
All the chords share the same scale notes, so moving between them feels coherent. The ear has heard these relationships for centuries and expects them. Breaking them (using non-diatonic chords) creates surprise — which is powerful when intentional (see §§15–17).

### Transposing
Once you know a progression as numerals, you can play it in any key by mapping the numerals to that key's scale degrees. "I–IV–V in G" = G–C–D. "ii–V–I in F" = Gm–C–F. This is why Roman numerals (and the Nashville Number System) are the lingua franca of working musicians.

## 12. Cadences & Turnarounds

### Cadences
A **cadence** is a chord progression that ends a phrase — the musical equivalent of punctuation.

| Cadence | Progression | Effect |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| **Authentic (V–I)** | G7–C | "period" — full resolution, strongest |
| **Plagal (IV–I)** | F–C | "amen" — softer, gospel |
| **Half (any–V)** | C–G | comma — leaves you hanging |
| **Deceptive (V–vi)** | G7–Am | plot twist — resolves to a substitute |

### Turnarounds
A **turnaround** is a 1- or 2-bar progression at the end of a form that loops back to the top. Common jazz turnarounds:
- **I–vi–ii–V** (C–Am–Dm–G)
- **iii–vi–ii–V** (Em–A7–Dm–G) — often with secondary dominants
- **I–VI7–ii–V** (C–A7–Dm–G)
- Tritone-sub versions: **I–bIII7–bVI7–bII7** (C–E♭7–A♭7–D♭7) — chromatic descending

## 13. The Blues & Jazz Blues

### The basic 12-bar blues
A 12-bar form built on I, IV, V (all played as dominant 7 chords in jazz/blues). In **F**:

```
| F7 | F7 | F7 | F7 |  (4 bars tonic)
| B♭7| B♭7| F7 | F7 |  (4 bars: subdominant and back)
| C7 | B♭7| F7 | C7 |  (4 bars: turnaround)
```

The final V→I (C7→F7) is the turnaround that loops to the next chorus.

### Blues variations
- **Quick change:** bar 2 becomes IV (B♭7) instead of I — common in jazz blues.
- **Minor blues:** i7–iv7–i7–V7 (e.g., Dm7–Gm7–Dm7–A7), often with a minor iv.

### Jazz blues (bebop) additions
Jazz players enrich the basic blues with secondary dominants and ii–V movement:

1. **Bar 4 cadence to IV:** insert `iim7–V7` leading to IV (e.g., `Cm7–F7–B♭7`), or use `F7alt` / a tritone sub.
2. **Bar 6 — the #4 diminished:** `B♭7 → B°7 → F7` (the sharp-4 diminished passes back to I; gives "blues flavor"; used in Tenor Madness, Blue Monk, Straight No Chaser).
3. **Bar 6 — the four-minor (iv):** `B♭7 → B♭m7 → E♭7 → F7` (a iv–V7–I back to tonic; used in Coltrane/Parker blues like Blues for Alice).
4. **Bar 8–9 cadence to ii:** `F7 → Eø7–A7 → Dm7` (auxiliary cadence to the ii).
5. **Bars 9–10:** the `ii–V` (`Gm7–C7`) — the defining cadence of the key.
6. **Turnaround (bars 11–12):** `F7 → … → C7` (various: I–vi–ii–V, tritone subs, etc.).
7. **Parallel ii–V chains:** chromatic descending ii–V pairs (e.g., `B♭m7–E♭7 → Am7–D7 → A♭m7–D♭7…`) — a bebop vocabulary staple.

### Scales over blues
- Over the **tonic (I7) and IV7**: **Mixolydian** (the parent major scale a 5th up).
- Over the **#4 diminished (bar 6)**: take the Mixolydian of IV and raise the 4th → effectively the **harmonic minor of the key** (e.g., C harmonic minor over B°7 in F blues), or think of the diminished as resolving to a ii chord sitting inside the I7.
- Over **V7**: Mixolydian, or altered if it's resolving (especially to a minor).

## 14. Jazz Harmony I: The ii–V–I Progression

The **ii–V–I** is the most important progression in jazz — it's the engine of countless standards (Autumn Leaves, All The Things You Are, Tune Up…). In **C major**: **Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7**.

### Why it works
- **ii (Dm7)** = pre-dominant, starts moving away from tonic.
- **V7 (G7)** = dominant, builds maximum tension (tritone B–F).
- **I (Cmaj7)** = resolution, releases the tension.
- The roots move in **4ths** (D→G→C) — the strongest, most "circular" root motion (see circle of fifths).

### How to solo over it — the core approaches
1. **Arpeggios + parent scale.** Play the arpeggio (chord tones) of each chord, connecting them with notes from the parent scale (C major for all three). The arpeggios are the "important notes at that moment"; the scale fills in around them.
2. **Target notes (the key skill).** Think ahead to a strong note in the next chord and play a line that ends on it. The **3rd of the next chord** is the best target — especially a note that *wasn't* in the previous chord, so the change is audible. (e.g., aim for B — the 3rd of G7 — on beat 1 of the V7 bar.)
3. **Barry Harris / Joe Pass approach — "forget the ii":** treat ii–V–I as just **V–I**. Focus on the dominant resolution; let the ii be a suspension of the V. Fewer chords to think about; strong V–I resolution.
4. **Pat Martino / "ii = V" simplification:** treat the ii and the V as one sound. Dm7 and G7 share almost all their notes (Dm7 = D F A C; G7 = G B D F — they share D, F, and the A of Dm7 is the 9th of G). Play over the whole ii–V as if it were one G7 chord, landing on the V's sound.
5. **Superimpose a V7 sound early:** play G7 (Mixolydian or G7 arpeggio) over the Dm7 bar too — you're anticipating the dominant. The 3rd of G7 (B) over Dm7 becomes a colorful #11/13 sound.
6. **Altered V7 for more tension:** over the V7, switch to the altered scale (see §20) so the resolution to Imaj7 is maximally tense and dramatic. The altered notes (b9, #9, #5, b13) all resolve by half-step into chord tones of the I.
7. **Pentatonic shortcut:** D minor pentatonic (D F G A C) works over the whole ii–V–I in C — it outlines Dm7, colors G7 (F = b7, C = 11/sus), and sits inside Cmaj7. One scale, three chords.

### Common ways to play ii–V–I on guitar
- **Arpeggio connections:** play Dm7 arpeggio → G7 arpeggio → Cmaj7 arpeggio, keeping notes close together on the neck and leading each into the next.
- **3rd-to-7th voice leading:** the 3rd of Dm7 (F) becomes the 7th of G7 (F); the 3rd of G7 (B) becomes the 7th of Cmaj7 (B). These two notes (F and B) just swap places — the tritone flips. This is the smoothest possible connection and the basis of jazz voice leading (see §23).
- **Guide tones:** improvise using only the 3rds and 7ths of each chord — they define the harmony and connect automatically.

## 15. Jazz Harmony II: Secondary Dominants

A **secondary dominant** is a dominant 7 chord that tonicizes (temporarily points at) a chord other than the I. It's the simplest way to introduce chromaticism and forward motion.

### The rule
**Every major and minor chord in the key can be preceded by its own V7.** That V7 is the "secondary dominant," written **V7/x** ("five of x").

In C major:
- The V of ii (Dm) is **A7** → written V7/ii. Progression: A7 → Dm.
- The V of IV (F) is **C7** → V7/IV. Progression: C7 → F.
- The V of V (G) is **D7** → V7/V. Progression: D7 → G.
- The V of vi (Am) is **E7** → V7/vi. Progression: E7 → Am.

### Why it works
A secondary dominant borrows one note from outside the key to create a temporary leading tone that pulls toward its target. The target chord is still diatonic, so the listener's ear accepts the detour and returns home. It's "chromatic spice" that intensifies the arrival on a diatonic chord.

### The classic example
"I–vi–ii–V" becomes "I–**V7/vi**–**V7/ii**–V" = C–**E7**–**A7**–Dm–G. The secondary dominants chain into each other, creating a strong sense of forward motion (each dominant resolves down a 5th / up a 4th to the next).

### Extended secondary dominants: ii–V of a target
You can also precede a target chord with a full **ii–V** in its key: e.g., to approach IV (F), play **Gm7–C7–F** (the ii–V of F). These "auxiliary cadences" are everywhere in jazz (e.g., bar 4 of a jazz blues, approaching the IV).

## 16. Jazz Harmony III: Tritone Substitution

**Tritone substitution** ("tritone sub") is the technique of replacing a dominant 7 chord with another dominant 7 whose root is a **tritone (6 semitones / 3 whole steps) away**. The classic: replace **G7** with **D♭7** in a ii–V–I → **Dm7–D♭7–Cmaj7**.

### Why it works — the magic of guide tones
A dominant 7 chord is defined by its **guide tones**: the 3rd and 7th. Look at G7 vs D♭7:

| Chord | Root | 3rd | 7th |
|-------|------|-----|-----|
| G7 | G | **B** | **F** |
| D♭7 | D♭ | **F** | **B (C♭)** |

The **3rd and 7th are the same two notes, just swapped.** Because the guide tones are identical, the two chords serve the same harmonic function — they both want to resolve to C. The roots (G and D♭) are a tritone apart, which splits the octave in half, so either root works with those guide tones.

### What it sounds like
- Instead of the roots going D–G–C (up a 4th, up a 4th), they go D–**D♭**–C — a smooth **chromatic descending bass line**. This is the "2–♭2–1" sound, very jazzy and sleek.
- It's also related to the classical **Neapolitan sixth** chord (a ♭II approaching I), which is where the technique ultimately comes from.

### When to use it
- Anytime you have a V7 (or a ii–V) resolving down a fifth, you can swap the V7 for its tritone sub. The result is more chromatic and "modern."
- **Tritone sub of the V7 of the V** (sub for V7/V) is especially common in turnarounds.
- You can even sub the ii: instead of Dm7–G7, use **A♭m7–D♭7** (the tritone-sub ii–V), giving a fully chromatic approach.

### The shortcut for altered dominants
The altered scale over G7 (G A♭ B♭ B C♯ E F — see §20) is ** enharmonically the same notes** as the D♭ Mixolydian / Lydian dominant scale over D♭7. So **"play the tritone sub's scale over the original V7" = "play altered."** This is why jazz teachers say: *the altered scale is just the Mixolydian of the tritone-sub dominant.*

## 17. Jazz Harmony IV: Borrowed Chords & Modal Mixture

**Modal mixture** (or "borrowed chords") means taking chords from the **parallel minor** (or another mode) and using them in a major key. This adds emotional color — usually a touch of sadness or darkness — without changing the tonal center.

### The parallel minor's chords (borrowable into C major)
The C natural minor scale (C D E♭ F G A♭ B♭) produces these chords, which can be borrowed into C major:

| Borrowed chord | In C | Common use | Effect |
|----------------|------|-----------|--------|
| **i (minor I)** | Cm | deceptive, color | "now it's sad" |
| **bIII** | E♭ | bridge to IV | broad, filmic |
| **iv (minor iv)** | Fm | ending a phrase to I | bittersweet, "Beatles" |
| **bVI** | A♭ | dramatic lift | epic, theatrical |
| **bVII** | B♭ | back-door to I | "Mixolydian" rock sound |
| **V7 from harmonic minor** | G7 (already diatonic, but the B♭ borrowing gives b7 color) | — | — |

### The most common borrowed chords
- **Minor iv (iv → I):** the bittersweet ending. Fm → C instead of F → C. The A♭ of Fm resolves down to G (5th of C). Heard in countless Beatles, jazz, and pop ballads.
- **bVI → bVII → I ("back-door" / Aeolian cadence):** A♭ → B♭ → C. A majestic, "final" sound (Radiohead, film scores). The bVII is also the "back-door dominant" — a iv–bVII–I cadence (Fm7–B♭7–Cmaj7) is a classic jazz ending.
- **bIII:** E♭ major appearing in C major — used for a moment of darker color before returning.

### Why it works
The parallel minor shares the same tonic note, so borrowed chords feel related but colored. They momentarily "darken" the major key without leaving it. This is the harmonic basis of much pop, rock, and jazz color.

## 18. Jazz Harmony V: The Minor ii–V–i

The minor ii–V–i is the minor-key counterpart of the major ii–V–I, and it's essential jazz vocabulary (Autumn Leaves, Blue Bossa, Black Orpheus). In **A minor**: **Bm7♭5 – E7♭9 – Am7**.

### The structure
| Chord | Roman | Quality | Notes (A minor) |
|-------|-------|---------|-----------------|
| Bm7♭5 | iiø7 | Half-diminished | B D F A |
| E7(♭9) | V7 | Dominant 7 (altered) | E G♯ B D **F** |
| Am7 (or Am6) | i7 | Minor 7 | A C E G |

### Why these chords
- **ii = m7♭5 (half-diminished):** built on the ii of the natural minor scale (B D F A). This is the diatonic ii of the minor key — it's naturally half-diminished.
- **V7 with alterations (♭9, ♭13):** the V comes from **harmonic minor** (E7 = E G♯ B D, with the G♯ raised leading tone). Players add ♭9 (F) and ♭13 (C) — notes from the harmonic/Aeolian minor scale — to heighten the dark, tense pull toward the minor tonic.
- **i = m7 or m6:** Am7 (A C E G) is the natural resolution; Am6 (A C E F♯) is a common variant (the 6th, F♯, is the Dorian color and a great melody note).

### Scales over the minor ii–V–i
- **Over iiø7 (Bm7♭5):** **Locrian** (7th mode of C major: B C D E F G A B), or **Locrian ♮2** (6th mode of D melodic minor) for a less dissonant sound.
- **Over V7 (E7alt):** the **altered scale** (7th mode of F melodic minor: E F G A♭ B♭ C D♭ E), or **E Phrygian dominant** (5th mode of A harmonic minor: E F G♯ A B C D E), or **E half-whole diminished**.
- **Over i (Am):** **Aeolian** (natural minor), **Dorian** (for the brighter 6th), or **harmonic minor** (for the leading-tone color).

### The minor ii–V–i "lick" sound
Because the V7 is altered and resolves to a minor tonic, the characteristic licks emphasize the altered tones resolving by half-step into chord tones of the tonic (Am = A C E G). For E7♭9 → Am:
- **♭9 (F) → E** (5th of Am) — down a semitone
- **♭5 (B♭) → A** (root of Am) — down a semitone
- **#9 (G)** is a common tone with Am (its 7th) — it colors the resolution.
- **#5/♭13 (C)** is a common tone with Am (its 3rd) — it colors the resolution.

Target the ♭9→5th and ♭5→root half-step descents for the most authentic minor-jazz "landing" sound.

## 19. Chord-Scale Theory: Scales Over Every Chord

**Chord-scale theory** (CST) is the jazz approach to improvisation: for each chord, identify the scale that contains its chord tones, then improvise with that scale. The chord is the "vertical" snapshot; the scale is the "horizontal" palette you draw lines from.

### The principle
Every chord is a vertical slice of one or more scales. If you know which scale fits a chord, you know which notes will sound consonant (chord tones + extensions) and which will add tension (avoid/passing notes).

### Scales for the common chord types

| Chord | Primary scale(s) | Notes (C root) |
|-------|------------------|----------------|
| Cmaj7 | **Ionian** (major) / **Lydian** | C D E F G A B / C D E F♯ G A B |
| Cmaj7(#11) | **Lydian** | C D E F♯ G A B |
| C6 / Cmaj6 | Ionian / major pentatonic | C D E F G A B / C D E G A |
| C7 (dominant) | **Mixolydian** | C D E F G A B♭ |
| C9, C13 | Mixolydian (add 9, 13) | C D E F G A B♭ |
| C7(#11) | **Lydian dominant** (4th mode melodic minor) | C D E F♯ G A B♭ |
| C7alt (b9/#9/b5/#5/b13) | **Altered scale** (7th mode melodic minor) | C D♭ E♭ F♭(E) G♭ A♭ B♭ |
| C7 (diminished sound) | **Half-whole diminished** | C D♭ E♭ E F♯ G A B♭ |
| C7 (whole-tone sound) | **Whole tone** | C D E F♯ G♯ A♯ |
| Cm7 | **Dorian** (preferred) / Aeolian | C D E♭ F G A B♭ / C D E♭ F G A♭ B♭ |
| Cm9, Cm11 | Dorian | C D E♭ F G A B♭ |
| Cm6 | **Melodic minor** / Dorian | C D E♭ F G A B |
| Cm7♭5 (half-dim) | **Locrian** / Locrian ♮2 | C D♭ E♭ F G♭ A♭ B♭ / C D E♭ F G♭ A♭ B♭ |
| Cdim7 | **Octatonic (W-H diminished)** | C D E♭ F G♭ A♭ A B |

### "Avoid" notes (a nuanced view)
Some scale degrees clash slightly with a chord and are best used as passing tones:
- **Natural 4 over a major 7** (the 4th is a half-step below the 3rd → "avoid" note in Ionian; use **Lydian** with #4 instead for a consonant 11th).
- **Natural 11 over a major or dominant chord** (clashes with the 3rd; use #11 / Lydian dominant instead).
- These aren't "wrong" — they're tension notes. Use them on purpose, resolved by step.

### The "chord tones are king" principle
Chord-scale theory gives you the palette, but **chord tones on strong beats** are what make a line outline the harmony. Use the scale to connect arpeggios; aim arpeggio tones (especially 3rds and 7ths) onto downbeats and chord changes.

## 20. The Altered Scale & Altered Dominants

The **altered scale** is the go-to scale for altered dominant chords (7alt, 7♭9, 7♯9, 7♭5, 7♯5, 7♭13). It contains **every possible alteration** of a dominant chord: b9, #9, b5, #5 (plus the root, 3rd, b7). Maximum tension, all resolving by half-step into the tonic.

### The formula
The altered scale = **7th mode of the melodic minor scale**.
For **G7alt**, the parent melodic minor is **A♭ melodic minor** (A♭ B♭ C D♭ E♭ F G); its 7th mode starting on G is:

```
G  A♭(b9)  B♭(#9)  B(3)  D♭(b5/#11)  E♭(#5/b13)  F(b7)  G
```

So: **1 b9 #9 3 b5 #5 b7** — every alteration of the 5th and 9th, plus the defining 3rd and b7th.

### Three rules for building the altered scale (recap)
1. It is the **7th mode of melodic minor** a half-step above the root (G7alt → A♭ melodic minor).
2. It is the **scale built from the root with b9, #9, b5, #5, plus 3 and b7** (the "everything altered" scale).
3. It equals the **Mixolydian of the tritone-sub dominant** (G7alt's altered scale = D♭ Mixolydian/Lydian dominant — the tritone sub of G7). So "altered = play the tritone sub's scale."

### How it resolves (the magic)
Every altered note resolves by **half-step** into a chord tone of the tonic. For **G7alt → Cmaj7** (C E G B):
- **b9 (A♭) → G** (5th of C) — down a semitone
- **#9 (B♭) → B** (3rd of Cmaj7) — up a semitone
- **#5 / ♭13 (E♭) → E** (3rd of Cmaj7) — up a semitone
- **b5 (D♭) → C** (root of C) — down a semitone

For a **minor tonic** (G7alt → Cm7 = C E♭ G B♭), the ♭9→5th and ♭5→root resolutions still apply, while #9 (B♭) and #5/♭13 (E♭) become **common tones** with Cm7 (its 7th and 3rd respectively). Either way, every altered note has a close, clear landing spot.

This half-step resolution is why altered dominants sound so tense-and-satisfying — every tension note has a clear, close landing spot.

### When to use it
- Over any **V7 resolving to a I (or i)**, especially in ii–V–I and minor ii–V–i.
- When the chart says **"7alt," "7♭9," "7♯9," "7♭13"** or **"+7#9"**.
- Not over a static/blues tonic dominant (there it's not resolving, so altered notes sound wrong — use Mixolydian).

## 21. Symmetrical Scales: Diminished & Whole Tone

These two symmetrical scales are specialized tools for dominant tension and chromatic motion.

### The diminished (octatonic) scale
Two forms, both 8 notes alternating whole/half or half/whole steps:

- **Half–Whole (H–W)** used over dominant 7 chords (C H–W: C D♭ E♭ E F♯ G A B♭). Contains 1, ♭9, #9, 3, #11, 5, 13, ♭7 — a dominant with b9 and #9 (and natural 5/13). Great over 7♭9/7#9 resolving to I.
- **Whole–Half (W–H)** used over diminished 7 chords (C W–H: C D E F G♭ A♭ A B). Contains the dim7 chord tones plus passing notes.

**Diminished symmetry:** because it's built from minor 3rds, **the same diminished scale/chord repeats every 3 frets.** There are really only 3 distinct diminished 7 chords (and 3 distinct diminished scales): C dim7 = E♭ dim7 = F♯ dim7 = A dim7 (they share the same 4 notes). This means you can move any diminished lick up or down 3 frets and it's the "same" sound — a powerful improvisational tool.

**Common uses:**
- **Passing diminished:** slide a dim7 chord between two diatonic chords a whole step apart (e.g., C → C♯dim7 → Dm) for chromatic bass motion.
- **Over 7♭9:** the H–W diminished scale starting on the root.
- **Diminished licks:** repeating 4-note patterns (minor 3rds) moved in minor-3rd cycles — a staple of bebop vocabulary.

### The whole tone scale
Six notes, all whole steps: C D E F♯ G♯ A♯ (C). Contains 1, 2, 3, #11, #5, b7 — a dominant with #5 and #11, no natural 5. Used over **+7 / 7#5 / 7#11** chords and for a "dreamy, floating, unresolved" dominant sound (Debussy, augmented chords, certain jazz turnarounds).

**Whole tone symmetry:** only **2 distinct whole-tone scales** exist (C D E F♯ G♯ A♯ and D♭ E♭ F G A B). Anything else is a transposition of one of these two.

## 22. Guitar Voicings: Shell, Drop 2, Drop 3, Quartal

Chords can be voiced many ways on guitar. Jazz guitarists use a vocabulary of specific voicing **systems** that maximize playability and smooth voice leading.

### Shell voicings
The **shell** = root + 3rd + 7th (the three notes that define the chord's quality and function — the 5th is usually omitted because it adds little). On guitar, shells are typically 3-note shapes using strings 6-4-2 or 5-3-1, leaving room to add an extension on top.

- Why they matter: they're **easy, mobile, and they voice-lead perfectly**. For a ii–V–I, the 3rd of one chord becomes the 7th of the next, so you only move one or two fingers.
- Add a 9th or 13th on the remaining string for instant jazz color.

### Drop 2 voicings
A **Drop 2** voicing is built from a closed-position 7th chord (4 notes stacked in thirds) by taking the **2nd-highest note and dropping it an octave** down. This spreads the chord across 4 adjacent strings (e.g., strings 6-5-4-3 or 5-4-3-2 or 4-3-2-1) in a playable, open-sounding shape.

- For each 7th-chord type (maj7, dom7, m7, m7♭5, dim7), there are **4 inversions** of the Drop 2 voicing (root, 1st, 2nd, 3rd), each on 3 string sets = a complete vocabulary of ~60 voicings.
- Drop 2 voicings **voice-lead smoothly** between chords in a progression — adjacent voicings often share 2 notes and move only 2 notes by step. This is the bread-and-butter of jazz comping.

### Drop 3 voicings
A **Drop 3** voicing takes the **3rd-highest note of the closed chord and drops it an octave**. This creates a voicing with a **skip between the lowest and next note**, typically played on strings 6-4-3-2 or 5-3-2-1 (a string-skip). Drop 3 voicings sound **bigger and more open** (the low bass note is separated from the upper structure) and are great when you want a fuller, lower sound or when comping behind a melody.

### When to use which
- **Shells:** fast, simple comping; walking-bass-plus-chord situations; beginners learning ii–V–I voice leading.
- **Drop 2:** the default for ensemble comping — full but not muddy, smooth voice leading on adjacent strings.
- **Drop 3:** fuller, lower register; duo/bass-less situations; when you want a richer, more orchestral sound.
- **Quartal (below):** modal vamps, modern jazz, "So What" sound.

### Learning strategy
Learn Drop 2 on the middle string set (5-4-3-2) first, all 4 inversions, for maj7, dom7, m7. That's 12 shapes that will cover most comping. Then expand to the other string sets and chord types, then add Drop 3.

## 23. Voice Leading

**Voice leading** is the art of moving from one chord to the next with the **least unnecessary motion** — each "voice" (note) moves by the smallest possible interval, ideally a step. Good voice leading makes a progression sound smooth, connected, and professional; bad voice leading sounds jumpy and amateur.

### The golden rules
1. **Keep common tones.** If two chords share a note, keep that finger where it is.
2. **Move the other voices by step** (half or whole), not by leap.
3. **Resolve tendency tones** in their natural direction: the 3rd of V7 (leading tone) resolves up to the tonic; the 7th of V7 resolves down by step.
4. **The 3rd and 7th (guide tones) are paramount.** They define the chord; the 5th (and often the root) can be omitted.

### The ii–V–I guide-tone miracle
In C: Dm7 (D F A C) → G7 (G B D F) → Cmaj7 (C E G B).
- The **3rd of Dm7 (F) becomes the 7th of G7 (F)** — same note, stays put.
- The **7th of Dm7 (C) steps down to B** (3rd of G7).
- The **3rd of G7 (B) becomes the 7th of Cmaj7 (B)** — same note, stays put.
- The **7th of G7 (F) steps down to E** (3rd of Cmaj7).

So across the whole ii–V–I, two notes (F and B) just **swap which is the 3rd and which is the 7th** — they barely move. This is why guide-tone comping (playing only 3rds and 7ths) is so effective: the harmony is fully defined and the voice leading is automatic.

### Voice leading on guitar
- Use **Drop 2 / Drop 3 inversions** chosen so that each chord is as close as possible to the previous one (same region of the neck).
- **Inversions are the tool:** by choosing the right inversion, you keep common tones common and stepwise motion smooth.
- **Tritone sub voice leading:** Dm7 → D♭7 → Cmaj7. The D♭7's guide tones (F and C♭/B) are the same as G7's (F and B) — so the tritone sub voice-leads just as smoothly, with the bonus of a chromatic descending bass (D → D♭ → C).

## 24. Modal Jazz & Quartal Harmony

**Modal jazz** (Miles Davis *Kind of Blue*, Coltrane, Wayne Shorter) uses **modes as tonal centers** rather than functional ii–V–I harmony. Instead of fast-moving chord changes, there's often a single chord/scale per section, and the interest comes from **melodic color and texture**, not harmonic motion.

### Key features
- **Few chords, held for many bars** (e.g., "So What": 16 bars of Dm7, 8 of E♭m7, 8 of Dm7).
- **"Vamps"** — a repeated 1- or 2-chord pattern over which the soloist explores a mode.
- The harmony is **non-functional** — there's no V7→I tension/release cycle; the chord just "is" its mode.

### Quartal harmony (chords in fourths)
Traditional harmony stacks **thirds** (1-3-5-7). **Quartal harmony** stacks **fourths**: 1-4-7-10(3)… On guitar, stacked-fourth voicings are easy and sound modern, open, and ambiguous.

- **The "So What chord":** a 5-note voicing of stacked fourths with one major third on top, e.g., for Dm7: D-G-C-F-A (low to high) = three perfect 4ths + a major 3rd. This is the signature sound of modal jazz.
- Why fourths? Because the **modes** that modal jazz uses (especially Dorian) are rich in perfect 4ths, and stacked-4th voicings naturally express the mode without strongly asserting a major/minor quality. They sound "floaty" and modern.

### Quartal voicings on guitar
- On the middle strings (4-3-2-1 or 5-4-3-2), stack perfect 4ths: e.g., E-A-D-G or F-B♭-E♭-A♭. These shapes are movable and easy to finger.
- **Parallel motion:** move a quartal voicing in parallel up and down the mode/scale — a defining gesture of modal comping (e.g., comping parallel 4th chords through D Dorian).
- Quartal voicings are **ambiguous** — the same shape can be interpreted as several different chords depending on the bass note. This ambiguity is a feature, not a bug, in modal contexts.

### The modes most used in modal jazz
- **Dorian** (So What, Little B's Poem, Maiden Voyage, Footprints' minor sections)
- **Mixolydian** (vamps on a dominant chord, "Maiden Voyage" sus chords)
- **Phrygian / Phrygian dominant** (Spanish/Middle-Eastern vamps)
- **Lydian** (floating major vamps, "Maiden Voyage" sus sections)

## 25. Harmonic & Melodic Minor: The Jazz Toolkits

Beyond the major scale and its modes, jazz relies heavily on the **harmonic minor** and **melodic minor** scales and their modes. Each mode of these scales fits a specific chord type.

### The modes of harmonic minor
Harmonic minor = 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 7. Its most important modes:

| Mode | Degree | Formula | Use |
|------|--------|---------|-----|
| Harmonic minor | I | 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 7 | minor tonic with leading tone |
| **Phrygian dominant** | V | 1 b2 3 4 5 b6 b7 | V7 → minor tonic (flamenco, "Spanish") |
| Locrian ♮6 / Locrian ♯13 | VII | 1 b2 b3 4 b5 6 b7 | rare; over vii° in minor |

### The modes of melodic minor (jazz)
Melodic minor (jazz) = 1 2 b3 4 5 6 7. All **seven** modes are used in jazz:

| Mode | Degree | Formula | Name / Use |
|------|--------|---------|------------|
| **Melodic minor** | I | 1 2 b3 4 5 6 7 | minor tonic (i m6); "jazz minor" |
| Dorian b2 / Phrygian ♮6 | II | 1 b2 b3 4 5 6 b7 | sus♭9 chords; rare |
| Lydian augmented | III | 1 2 3 #4 #5 6 7 | +maj7 chords; rare |
| **Lydian dominant** | IV | 1 2 3 #4 5 6 b7 | 7#11, tritone subs, blues IV7 |
| Mixolydian b6 / Hindu | V | 1 2 3 4 5 b6 b7 | rare dominant |
| **Locrian ♮2** | VI | 1 2 b3 4 b5 b6 b7 | m7♭5 (less dissonant than Locrian) |
| **Altered (super Locrian)** | VII | 1 b2 b3 3 b5 b6 b7 | 7alt, altered dominants (see §20) |

The **four bolded modes** (melodic minor, Lydian dominant, Locrian ♮2, Altered) are the most-used. They cover: minor tonics, 7#11/tritone-sub dominants, half-diminished chords, and altered dominants — the core altered-harmony toolkit.

### Why melodic minor is a jazz secret weapon
Every mode of melodic minor contains the **perfect 4th and perfect 5th** (no "avoid" notes), so all the notes are consonant with the chord. This is why melodic minor modes are so usable for improvisation — there's no "wrong" note, only choices of color. By contrast, the major-scale modes have avoid notes (the 4 in Ionian, the 11 in Mixolydian, etc.).

## 26. Reharmonization & Substitutions Overview

**Reharmonization** is the art of changing the chords under a melody to create a new (often richer or more surprising) harmonic context. Jazz musicians reharmonize in real time. The main techniques, all covered above:

| Technique | What it does | Section |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Secondary dominants** | Insert V7/x to tonicize a diatonic chord | §15 |
| **Tritone substitution** | Replace V7 with ♭II7 for chromatic bass | §16 |
| **Borrowed chords / modal mixture** | Pull chords from parallel minor for color | §17 |
| **Chord-quality substitution** | Swap a chord for a related quality (e.g., maj7↔m7 via relative) | §8 |
| **Diminished passing chords** | Slide dim7 between diatonic chords for chromatic motion | §21 |
| **Planing / parallel motion** | Move identical chord shapes in parallel (often quartal or triadic) through a scale | §24 |
| **Reharmonizing the melody** | Choose a new chord whose notes include or color the melody note | — |

### Principles
- **Preserve the melody:** the melody note should remain consonant (or intentionally tensional) with the new chord.
- **Preserve the function:** a sub should usually keep the chord's role (e.g., a tritone sub for V7 still functions as a dominant).
- **Voice-lead smoothly:** the best reharmonizations also move the inner voices gracefully.
- **More changes ≠ better:** reharmonize where it adds meaning, not everywhere.

## 27. Recommended Learning Path

A pedagogically sound sequence for an experienced guitarist with no harmony grounding:

### Phase 1 — Foundations (the fretboard & intervals)
1. Learn the 12 notes; memorize notes on strings 6 and 5 up to fret 12.
2. Learn octave shapes to find any note anywhere.
3. Master the 12 intervals by semitone count; sing/play them against a root.
4. Internalize the major-scale W-W-H-W-W-W-H pattern in multiple keys.

### Phase 2 — Chords & diatonic harmony
5. Build the 4 triad qualities (maj, min, dim, aug) from formulas; play them as small shapes on all string sets.
6. Learn triad inversions across the neck.
7. Build the 5 seventh chords (maj7, dom7, m7, m7♭5, dim7) from formulas.
8. Derive the 7 diatonic chords of a major key (the maj-m-min-maj-maj-min-dim sequence); practice in several keys.
9. Learn Roman numerals and transpose simple progressions (I–IV–V, I–vi–IV–V).

### Phase 3 — Functional harmony & the circle
10. Study tonic/subdominant/dominant functions; hear V7→I resolution.
11. Learn the circle of fifths: key signatures, I–IV–V, relative minor, transposition.
12. Learn the common cadences (authentic, plagal, half, deceptive).
13. Play the 12-bar blues and a few pop/rock progressions in multiple keys.

### Phase 4 — Scales, modes & extensions
14. Learn natural, harmonic, and melodic minor; pentatonic and blues scales.
15. Learn the 7 modes of the major scale (construct from parent scale, then as parallel formulas); identify characteristic notes.
16. Build extended chords (9, 11, 13) and altered dominants (♭9, ♯9, ♯11, ♭13) on shells.

### Phase 5 — Jazz harmony
17. Master the ii–V–I (major): comp it, arpeggiate it, voice-lead the guide tones, solo with target notes.
18. Learn secondary dominants and insert them into progressions.
19. Learn tritone substitution; play ii–♭II–I.
20. Learn borrowed chords (minor iv, bVI–bVII–I back-door).
21. Master the minor ii–V–i with altered V7.
22. Study chord-scale theory: match scales to every chord type.
23. Learn the altered scale and symmetrical (diminished/whole-tone) scales.
24. Learn Drop 2 and Drop 3 voicings; comp through standards with smooth voice leading.

### Phase 6 — Advanced jazz
25. Study modal jazz and quartal harmony (So What voicings, parallel fourths).
26. Learn the modes of melodic and harmonic minor (Lydian dominant, altered, Phrygian dominant, Locrian ♮2).
27. Analyze and reharmonize jazz standards; apply substitutions in context.
28. Transcribe solos by jazz guitarists (Wes Montgomery, Joe Pass, Pat Martino, Jim Hall) to absorb vocabulary.

## 28. Pentatonics & Blue Notes in Practice

The pentatonic and blues scale *formulas* live in §3. This section covers their **musical usage** — the difference between running a scale and making music, and what a blue note actually *is*.

### The pentatonic is material, not a ladder
The pentatonic scale (major: 1 2 3 5 6; minor: 1 ♭3 4 5 ♭7) is the safest scale there is — five notes, no half steps, almost no "wrong" choices. That safety is also its trap: playing it up and down is not music. The sound of blues, jazz, and all Black American music comes from **embellishment** (turns, slides, grace notes, bends, hammer-ons/pull-offs) and from **blue notes** slipped between the scale tones.

### What a blue note is
A **blue note** is a *chromatic passing note* — a note outside the chord/diatonic scale, wedged between two notes that belong. The most famous is the **♭5** (a.k.a. ♯4) in a minor-pentatonic context, which turns the minor pentatonic into the **blues scale** (1 ♭3 4 ♭5 5 ♭7). But there are many others: the ♭3 leaned against a major chord, the ♭7, the ♯9, or a quarter-tone bend between the major and minor 3rd.

### The golden rule: a blue note is a passing note
What makes a note "blue" is not *which* note it is but **how it behaves**: a blue note is a **passing note you never rest on.** You touch it for an instant on the way to a chord tone a semitone above or below, and the *resolution* is what makes it sound blue. Land on a blue note and stop, and it stops sounding blue and starts sounding wrong — the tension only means something because it resolves.

### The harmony under a blue note
Melody and harmony mirror each other. When the melody leans on a blue note, the chord underneath is very often a **diminished 7** — one of the symmetric passing chords from §21. The blue note (melodic chromatic tension) and the dim7 (harmonic chromatic tension) are two sides of the same coin: both slide by half-step into a chord tone. This is why a blues solo over a passing dim7 sounds so cohesive.

### Practical approach
- **Don't run the scale.** Use two or three notes, then slide off one through a blue note into the next.
- **Approach chord tones from a semitone below** (e.g., approach C via B/C♭).
- **Bend into the note.** A quarter-tone bend up to the ♭3 or ♭5 is the most vocal, human sound the guitar makes; the major/minor ambiguity *is* the blues.
- **Resolve, always.** Every blue note resolves by half-step into a chord tone. The resolution is the blues; the blue note is the set-up.

## 29. Associative Learning: Inversions, Phrases & How to Practice

This section distills a Chema Vílchez masterclass on *how to study* rather than *what to study*. Its thesis: most players stall not from lack of information but from **mecanicidad** — mechanical, disconnected repetition (running a scale or arpeggio up and down until the fingers move fast but the ear, eye, and mind are not engaged). The way out is a **multisensory, associative** method that stores every learned thing along several paths at once.

### The four memories
Practise so that each thing you learn is recorded in more than one kind of memory:

- **Auditory** — you can *hear* the note or line in your head before you play it. Train it by **singing what you play**: play slowly and voice each note. If you cannot sing it, you do not yet *know* it.
- **Visual** — you *see* the whole pattern on the neck, not just the note under your finger. Picture the full scale/chord shape and practise **jumping intervals** (leaping to a distant scale tone) instead of always stepping to the neighbouring degree.
- **Muscular** — your hands know the shape with the **minimum tension**. Practise relaxed; use only the force needed (the "bottle at 10% of your grip" image). Tension practised in is tension played out, and is the enemy of speed, tone, and stamina — the "effortless mastery" that looks like talent but is really relaxed, well-trained hands.
- **Associative** — each new thing is **glued to something already known**, so it is never isolated. Derive, do not memorise.

### Associative chord learning: 20 voicings from one seed
Take a single chord (e.g. **Cmaj7** = C E G B) on a string set (5-4-3-2) and apply two repeated moves:

1. **Rotate the bass tone** — put each chord tone in the bass in turn (root, 3rd, 5th, 7th). Same four notes, four inversions. Five voicings (root + 4 inversions) for the price of one.
2. **Hop a note to a neighbour string** — move the top note (e.g. the 7th on string 2) to string 1 (same pitch class, an octave up). The chord is unchanged but now lives on a new string set (5-4-3-1), spawning a *second* family of four inversions.

Repeat the hop on other tones and each rearrangement opens another string set (5-4-3-1, 5-4-2-1, 5-3-2-1, 6-4-3-2…). **Four inversions × a handful of string sets ≈ twenty voicings, all derived from the one seed** — not twenty things to remember, but one method applied twenty times. Since the movable forms have no open strings, the whole set transposes up the neck unchanged: one set of twenty, twelve places.

### The phrase dictionary
Improvisers are not inventing every note — they recombine an assimilated **vocabulary of phrases** (short, learned melodic units), each anchored to a scale *position*. Build your own dictionary: when a phrase catches your ear, notate it and **label the position it lives in**. Then **transpose the same fingering** to other positions (same fingers, same shape, new key) — one learned idea serves in every key. The discipline is to *vary and recombine*, not to hoard: ten variants of one connected idea beat ten unrelated licks, and a phrase stuck in one key is half a phrase.

### Why this maps to interactive widgets
A textbook can name a chord or scale; it cannot *sound* it, *light* it on the neck, or let you *move* it to every key. The multisensory method demands that the **sound, the shape, and the name arrive together** — see the note on the neck, hear it ring, find it under a finger, and voice it. Playable, clickable fretboard/piano widgets with per-note sounding highlights and transposition steppers are not decoration; they are the method, built into the lesson. They forge the audio-visual link that turns "I read it" into "I own it."

## 30. The Fretboard Landscape: Green Lights & Red Tonic

Most guitarists learn scales as "boxes" — five or seven finger patterns, each covering a four-fret region. They get fluent inside one box, then stall the moment a melodic line wants to travel higher or lower: the shape ends, the map blanks out, and the player either shifts clumsily to the next box or retreats to where they feel safe. Pat Metheny described a fundamentally different way of seeing the neck. When he improvises in a key, he does not see boxes at all — he sees **every available note in that scale, across the entire fretboard, lit up like green lights**, with the **tonic (the root) lit up red**. The neck is not a puzzle of interlocking shapes; it is one continuous landscape of green lights, with red lights marking home.

This single image dissolves three problems at once:

1. **Box confinement.** If the whole neck is lit, you are never trapped in four frets. You can move freely up and down because every green light is available — there is no "edge of the shape" to fall off.
2. **Stepwise playing.** Boxes train you to shuffle to the next-door note, which sounds like an exercise. When every available note is visible at once, you naturally pick interesting jumps — wide intervals that sound like music, not drills. **Jump, don't walk.**
3. **Losing the root.** The red lights are always on. Wherever you wander in the green field, you can see the nearest red light and resolve there. A solo feels lost when the player has lost the tonic; the red map prevents that.

### How to build the image

The landscape is built progressively, not absorbed all at once:

- **Map the red lights first.** Find every occurrence of the tonic on the fretboard — not by hunting, but by instant recall (the octave shapes from Lesson 1 are how the red lights connect across strings). These red anchors are the safety net: if you get lost mid-phrase, aim for the nearest red light and you are home.
- **Light up the green, one string at a time.** Play the scale up and down a *single string* — this teaches the intervals in a line, not a box. Repeat on each string until the green lights distribute themselves linearly in your mind.
- **Compare to the piano-key view.** A pianist in C major does not think in boxes — they see all the white keys laid out and can jump from a low C to a high G because the layout is obvious. The green-lights image turns the guitar neck into that same kind of keyboard: a single field of available notes, not a maze of shapes.
- **Jump, don't walk.** Pick any two lit spots and connect them directly — low C on string 6 up to high G on string 1, down to mid E on string 4. You can only make these jumps if you see the lights; if you see boxes, you are stuck walking.

### Why it transfers to every key

Cycle the root and the *entire landscape* re-colours — the green field shifts, the red anchors relocate, but the map stays one connected piece. You are not learning twelve sets of boxes; you are learning one landscape that relocates. The method of seeing — green field, red home — is invariant; only the colours change. This is why the green-lights image is taught immediately after the major scale (Lesson 4), before any box habits form: it gives every subsequent scale, mode, and pentatonic a single visual framework. When you learn minor scales or modes later, you do not learn new boxes — you ask *"where are the green lights now, and which one is red?"* The landscape changes colour; the method of seeing it stays the same.

## 31. The Diminished Blues Pentatonic

A recurring soloing question in blues and jazz: **what do you play over a passing diminished chord?** You are grooving on a D9, soloing in D major pentatonic, and the progression moves to a passing D♯dim7 a half-step up — there and gone in a beat or two. The reflex is to switch to a straight D♯ diminished arpeggio (D♯ F♯ A C). It is mathematically correct, but it sounds rigid and *square* — four equal chord tones with no blues in it. Félix Santos's alternative is not a new scale but a **treatment** of the pentatonic you were already playing.

### The construction

Keep the **major pentatonic of the preceding chord** (D major pentatonic: D E F♯ A B) under your fingers and treat the diminished root (D♯) as a **blues appoggiatura** — a note you slide into and immediately leave. The direction of the slide depends on the direction of the line:

- **Ascending** — slide into D♯ from a half-step *below* (D → D♯), and **omit the E** (the pentatonic note just above). The D♯ gets the slide emphasis; the clash note above it never sounds.
- **Descending** — flip the rule: **keep the E** and slide *down* into D♯ from a half-step *above* (E → D♯). The former clash note is now your approach note, so it earns its place.

The ascending line becomes D → D♯ → F♯ → A → B → D (the E dropped, the D♯ slid into from below). The descending line falls through the pentatonic — F♯ → E → D → B → A → F♯ → E — and only at the end slides E → D♯ (the dim root approached from above). Direction determines treatment: up = from below, down = from above.

### Why it works

A straight diminished arpeggio sounds classical because it is *all chord tones, all the time* — no passing tension, no slide. The blues, by contrast, is built on pentatonic shapes decorated with slides and half-step approaches. By keeping the pentatonic and redirecting one note into the diminished root, you get the harmonic tension the dim7 demands *without abandoning the blues vocabulary*.

The trick is harmonically grounded too: the pentatonic and the arpeggio overlap on the 3rd and 5th of the diminished chord (F♯ and A are chord tones of both D major pentatonic and D♯dim7). So most of the line is already "correct" over the dim7 — the only negotiation is the root itself, and that is exactly the note the slide rules handle.

### It generalises

This is not one position. It is a method: wherever a passing diminished chord appears, grab the major pentatonic of the chord right before it and apply the two slide rules. The same fingering relocates to any string set — an A groove moving to A♯dim7 uses the A major pentatonic shape with the same omit-above / keep-above rules, just shifted down a fourth. One method, every position.
